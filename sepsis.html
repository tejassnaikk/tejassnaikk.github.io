SepsisSentinel turns MIMIC-IV ICU data into a continuous sepsis-risk signal: it streams patient vitals through Kafka, lands them in Delta Lake, aggregates time-windowed features, scores them with XGBoost, and serves predictions via FastAPI on Railway — with a RAG layer that retrieves relevant clinical context. The data is large and imbalanced (~935K windows, ~25% sepsis), which shaped most of the engineering. It's a research prototype on a public research dataset, not a clinical tool.

The problem

ICU sepsis is time-critical — earlier warning saves lives. The modeling is the easy part; the hard part is building a streaming system over messy clinical time-series without leaking future information, handling a heavily imbalanced label, and serving continuously. Most takes on this are a single notebook with one offline number; the goal here was a running pipeline that scores patients in motion.

03

Key decisions & tradeoffs

The choices that show judgment — where the obvious approach wasn't the right one for clinical time-series.

Compute

DuckDB for Silver/Gold, not PySpark

The Silver/Gold aggregation originally ran on PySpark and ran out of memory building the ~935K-window Gold table. Moving the aggregations to in-process DuckDB fixed it within the same footprint. Same lesson that keeps recurring: when the data fits one node, distributed compute is overhead, not capability.

Features

Temporal lag & delta features

A single snapshot of vitals misses the trend, and in sepsis the trajectory is the signal. Adding lag and delta features (63 total) — how each vital is changing, not just its current value — pushed test AUROC from 0.806 to 0.810.

Editor note. Add the baseline this beats (e.g. a vitals-only model or a clinical score like SOFA/qSOFA) to make the number land.
Evaluation

Patient-level split + subgroup analysis

The split is by patient, not by row — otherwise windows from the same stay leak across train/test and quietly inflate AUROC. I also ran subgroup analysis across cohorts to check the model holds rather than reporting a single headline number, which matters before anyone trusts a clinical model.

Editor note. Confirm the split is strictly patient-level and name the subgroups you checked.
Model

XGBoost for tabular clinical signals

Clinical features are heterogeneous and missingness-prone; gradient-boosted trees handle that well, train fast, and give feature importances that a clinician can sanity-check. A deep net would add cost and opacity for no clear gain on this tabular data.

Context

A RAG layer for grounding

A risk score on its own is hard to act on. A retrieval layer (sentence-transformers embeddings, cosine similarity) surfaces relevant clinical reference text next to each prediction, turning a number into something a reviewer can reason about.
