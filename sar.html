01

The problem

Vessels that go dark — not broadcasting AIS — are of interest for maritime monitoring, and SAR can see them through cloud and darkness where optical sensors can't. But detecting small, arbitrarily-oriented ships in noisy radar is hard, and inshore clutter like port infrastructure easily fools a detector. The project builds an oriented-box detector and, more importantly, tests whether a model trained on one SAR dataset actually holds on real Sentinel-1 scenes.

03

Key decisions & tradeoffs

The choices that show this is engineering, not a leaderboard chase.

Task

Oriented bounding boxes (OBB)

Ships are elongated and arbitrarily oriented in SAR. Axis-aligned boxes would swallow background and merge vessels that lie close together; oriented boxes match the geometry of the target and give tighter, more separable detections.

Model

YOLOv11n — the nano variant

I chose the smallest variant deliberately: fast inference and a small footprint mean the model runs in a free HF Spaces demo and could plausibly run at the edge. The accuracy/size tradeoff favored nano for something deployable rather than a heavyweight model that only lives in a notebook.

Evaluation

Cross-domain test on hand-labeled Sentinel-1

Rather than report only the in-distribution RSDD-SAR mAP, I hand-labeled Sentinel-1 GRD scenes and evaluated there. That's where you find out whether a detector generalizes or just overfit its training distribution — and it surfaced real, specific failure modes (below).

Framing

Candidates, not confirmations

A scoping decision baked into the design: the model surfaces AIS-dark vessel candidates for a human to review. It can't establish intent or legality, and claiming it could would be both wrong and irresponsible. The honest framing is part of the engineering.

05

What broke, and what I'd do differently

The cross-domain evaluation is where the real findings are — and they're the most valuable part of the project.

  • Systematic offshore under-sizingOn Sentinel-1, predicted boxes covered only ~64% of ground-truth area — the regression head consistently undersizes offshore vessels out of domain.
  • Inshore false positivesPort infrastructure triggers false detections inshore; the offshore/inshore mAP gap widens further out of distribution.
  • The domain gap is realRSDD-SAR → Sentinel-1 GRD is a genuine distribution shift; the in-distribution mAP overstates real-world performance.
  • Next step is targeted, not wholesaleFine-tune the offshore box-regression head on 116 hand-labeled Sentinel-1 boxes — fix the specific failure rather than retrain everything.
